# Midwest Craft Distillery Self-Distribution Brief

Prepared for Sonat Birnecker Hart by Ezra Katz, Special Projects & Legal Affairs.
Research snapshot: June 16, 2026.

This is a counsel-ready business brief for issue spotting and legislative strategy. It is not final legal advice and should not be used as an external legal or regulatory position without attorney and owner approval.

## Practical Readout

Illinois is not the worst Midwest model for a small spirits producer, but its 5,000 gallon cap combines retail self-distribution and direct consumer/on-premise sales in a way that can pinch a distillery with a strong hospitality/tourism business. Better comparison points for KOVAL are Michigan, Nebraska, North Dakota, and the way some states separate distillery retail/tourism privileges from wholesale distribution. The cleanest legislative path is probably not "remove aggregate" by itself. A stronger framework is to separate three buckets: sales to retailers, sales at the distillery/tasting room, and tourism/event sales, with separate caps and records for each bucket.

## Scope

I treated "Midwestern states" as the U.S. Census Midwest region: Illinois, Indiana, Iowa, Kansas, Michigan, Minnesota, Missouri, Nebraska, North Dakota, Ohio, South Dakota, and Wisconsin. The first-pass comparison distinguishes:

- self-distribution to retailers for resale;
- on-premise/cocktail room/tasting room sales;
- off-premise consumer bottle sales from the distillery premises;
- tourism/event-style privileges;
- useful statutory hooks for an Illinois reform proposal.

## Better Models Than Illinois

1. Michigan: useful for a direct spirits self-distribution path, including out-of-state equivalent small distillers, but the cap is 3,000 gallons and the pricing/reporting framework is highly controlled.
2. Nebraska: useful because it expressly allows direct resale sales to retailers up to 3,500 gallons and separately gives microdistilleries on/off-premise retail privileges for their own product.
3. North Dakota: useful because it appears to allow a much larger domestic distillery direct-sale/direct-shipment bucket than Illinois, but it needs attorney verification before becoming a legislative model.
4. Kansas and South Dakota: useful for separating premises consumer sales from wholesaler sales, not for retailer self-distribution.
5. Indiana: useful only as a modest self-distribution comparison; its 2,000 gallon self-distribution cap is less helpful than Illinois' 5,000 gallon cap.

## Suggested Illinois Framework

The strongest KOVAL ask is to separate the current aggregate pressure into distinct policy buckets:

1. Retailer self-distribution bucket: keep a compliance-monitored annual cap for sales and deliveries to licensed Illinois retailers. This preserves the distributor lobby's main concern.
2. Distillery premises bucket: exclude bona fide on-premise and off-premise sales at the distillery, tasting room, cocktail room, and approved hospitality premises from the retailer self-distribution cap.
3. Tourism/event bucket: create a separate Malt Row/tourism/event privilege for sales tied to tourism venues, licensed events, educational tastings, and local economic-development districts, with its own reporting and limits.
4. Anti-abuse guardrails: require separate records, no distributor bypass for ordinary retail accounts above the cap, no shipping unless separately authorized, no sales to unlicensed retailers, and transparent annual reporting.
5. Local economic-development finding: frame KOVAL's Ravenswood/Malt Row activity as manufacturing tourism, not merely retail expansion.

## Draft Statutory Concept

Do not use this as filed language without counsel.

"For purposes of the annual self-distribution limitation applicable to a class 1 craft distiller, sales and deliveries to retail licensees shall be counted separately from bona fide sales to consumers for on-premises consumption or off-premises consumption from the licensed distillery premises, approved tasting room, approved cocktail room, or approved tourism/event premises. Consumer sales under this subsection shall not be deemed sales or deliveries to retail licensees, shall be recorded separately, and shall remain subject to all State Commission rules, local licensing requirements, tax reporting, age-verification requirements, and any premises-specific limits established by the Commission."

This avoids arguing that KOVAL should simply be uncapped. It gives legislators a middle position: protect the three-tier system for normal retailer sales while not treating distillery tourism revenue as if it were wholesale distribution.

## Questions For Counsel Or Owner Approval

- Should the Illinois target be only consumer/tasting-room sales excluded from the 5,000 gallon cap, or should KOVAL also seek a higher retailer self-distribution cap?
- Should the comparison include only contiguous/nearby Midwest states, or the full Census Midwest set used here?
- Should North Dakota's direct-sale/direct-shipment model be included in the lobbying packet after attorney verification, or left out because it may invite distributor opposition?
- Should the Malt Row/tourism argument be drafted as a general "manufacturing tourism district" privilege rather than a KOVAL-specific carveout?
