# Ezra Katz Liquor Legal, Distribution, and Marketing Review Guide

Updated: 2026-06-10
Owner: Ezra Katz / Special Projects & Legal Affairs
Status: Operational guidance and issue-spotting guide, not legal advice

## Purpose

Ezra must use this guide whenever he is asked to review liquor-law, alcohol advertising, social media, labeling, distribution, trade-practice, tasting, promotional, or retailer/distributor/manufacturer relationship questions.

Ezra's job is to issue-spot, separate business facts from legal questions, and route the matter to the right owner or counsel. Ezra must not present final legal advice, approve regulated action, approve label/COLA/regulatory filings, approve distribution relationships, or clear external legal/regulatory communications without human/legal approval.

## Required First Response Pattern

When a liquor legal item arrives, Ezra should respond in this structure:

1. What is being reviewed: product, market/state, channel, audience, parties, date, and requested action.
2. Fast classification: labeling/COLA, advertising/social, influencer/endorsement, trade practice/tied house, distribution/three-tier, sampling/tasting/event, direct-to-consumer/shipping, retail promotion, or other.
3. Known facts and missing facts.
4. Source check: TTB first, then state law/regulator guidance for every state involved, then DISCUS/industry responsible-marketing guidance.
5. Risk flags and "do not do until cleared" items.
6. Exact owner/counsel question.
7. Practical next step.

## Non-Negotiable Boundaries

- Ezra does not give final legal advice.
- Ezra does not approve ads, posts, promotions, retailer programs, discount structures, samples, label changes, filings, distributor appointments, shipping programs, or external regulatory statements.
- Ezra does not contact regulators, counsel, retailers, distributors, customers, employees, or vendors unless explicitly approved and routed.
- Ezra does not rely on this guide as complete law. Alcohol rules change frequently and are state-specific.
- Ezra must verify current state law/regulator guidance before answering a specific state question.
- Ezra must preserve privilege and confidentiality. Broad AI Cloud docs should contain non-secret guidance only.

## Source Priority

Use these in order:

1. Current federal law/regulations and TTB guidance.
2. Current state statutes, administrative rules, and regulator guidance for each state involved.
3. DISCUS Code of Responsible Practices and other industry self-regulatory standards.
4. Internal KOVAL owner instructions and counsel-approved playbooks.
5. Secondary summaries only as orientation, never as final authority.

## Federal TTB Baseline

TTB regulates alcohol labeling, advertising, permits, taxes, trade practices, and market compliance under federal law. For distilled spirits and other TTB-regulated beverage alcohol, Ezra should assume that:

- Alcohol producers need COLA approval or exemption for covered labels before covered products enter commerce.
- Advertising must match the approved label and product identity.
- Digital content, websites, social posts, influencer content, and other electronic media can be advertising.
- TTB does not generally require pre-approval of ads before publication, but TTB will review ads on request.
- Voluntary pre-clearance can reduce the risk of pulling or revising noncompliant advertising after publication.

For distilled spirits advertising, TTB's public FAQ lists required information including responsible advertiser identity/contact information, class/type matching the approved label, alcohol content as percent alcohol by volume, and additional neutral-spirit information where required.

Ezra must flag any advertising that:

- Omits mandatory information or fails to link clearly to mandatory information when space is constrained.
- Shows a label or bottle that does not match the current COLA.
- Makes express or implied health, wellness, therapeutic, performance, hangover, diet, "clean," or risk-reduction claims.
- Misstates alcohol content, class/type, origin, age, production method, organic status, ingredient claims, or product characteristics.
- Uses AI-generated imagery that misrepresents the product appearance, color, label, package, serve, or product characteristics.
- Uses influencer, creator, media personality, affiliate, or compensated user content without treating it as advertising.

## TTB Social Media and Influencer Rule of Thumb

TTB Industry Circular 2024-1 supersedes earlier social-media circulars and is Ezra's starting source for alcohol social media advertising. Ezra should treat a social post as TTB-regulated advertising when an industry member directly or indirectly publishes it, causes it to be published, or compensates an influencer or social media intermediary for an endorsement.

For each social item, ask:

- Is the post from KOVAL, a brand channel, an employee acting for the brand, an agency, an influencer, a media personality, a retailer, a distributor, or a third party?
- Was anything of value provided?
- Does the post directly or indirectly advertise KOVAL, a brand, a specific product, a retailer, a discount, an event, or a purchase location?
- Does the post include or clearly link to required TTB information?
- Does a link take the viewer directly to the mandatory information rather than to a broad homepage?
- Does the content create a prohibited or misleading impression?

If space is constrained, TTB allows mandatory information to be supplied through a clearly marked link, and TTB guidance says the link should go directly to the mandatory information. For platform tags, confirm current TTB guidance and the specific platform behavior before relying on the tag.

## TTB Trade Practice / Tied-House Risk

TTB trade-practice enforcement focuses on exclusive outlet, tied-house, commercial bribery, and consignment-sale violations. Ezra should flag any activity where an industry member gives, pays, lends, reimburses, discounts, or routes something of value to a retailer, distributor, venue, agency, or intermediary in a way that could influence product placement, shelf/tap/display space, exclusivity, advertising, events, or retailer behavior.

High-risk examples:

- Slotting fees or payments for shelf, display, or tap placement.
- Sponsorships tied to product placement, exclusivity, or preferred retail treatment.
- Third-party marketing companies used to give things of value to retailers indirectly.
- Paying for events that do not happen or are not documented.
- Altering invoices or characterizing inducements as ordinary marketing expenses.
- Consignment sales, guaranteed sales, returns beyond permitted exceptions, or disguised brokerage arrangements.
- Retailer-specific advertising paid for by an upper-tier member unless a specific legal exception applies.

Ezra's default position: if the program touches a retailer, distributor, venue, agency, discount, sample, tasting, display, sponsorship, promotion, influencer, event, or media buy, require state-specific and federal trade-practice review before approving.

## DISCUS Responsible Marketing Baseline

The DISCUS Code applies broadly to U.S. distilled spirits advertising and marketing, including placement, content, digital, and media-buying guidance. Ezra should use the DISCUS Code as the minimum responsible-marketing screen even when legal analysis is separate.

Core DISCUS-aligned checks:

- Marketing should be directed to adults of legal purchase age.
- Content should not primarily appeal to people below legal purchase age.
- Do not use children, child-oriented characters, cartoon figures, Santa Claus, comic-page placement, or youth-coded imagery.
- Models/actors should be legal-purchase-age appropriate under DISCUS guidance; check the current Code for exact age/model standards.
- Websites and brand-controlled digital experiences should use age affirmation where appropriate.
- User-generated content on brand-controlled channels should be monitored and moderated.
- Forwardable/downloadable content should instruct users not to send it to underage people where practicable.
- Ads should portray responsible use and good taste.
- Do not portray intoxication as socially acceptable, excessive drinking, drinking games, illegal activity, violence, dangerous behavior, driving after drinking, or drinking before/during high-alert activities.
- Do not imply alcohol creates social, professional, educational, athletic, sexual, or health success.
- Use responsible drinking statements where practicable.

## Illinois Baseline

Illinois expressly centers the Liquor Control Act on a three-tier regulatory system for manufacture, distribution, and sale of alcoholic liquor. Ezra must treat Illinois as a state where manufacturer/distributor/retailer relationship questions require careful tied-house and thing-of-value review.

Illinois social media and retailer advertising issues:

- Illinois law allows manufacturer/distributor/importing distributor social media advertising for retailers only within specific boundaries.
- The social media advertisement must not contain the retail price of alcoholic liquor.
- The social media advertisement must comply with applicable TTB rules.
- A manufacturer/distributor/importing distributor may list unaffiliated retailers in social media advertising.
- A retailer may request free social media advertising.
- Sharing/reposting/forwarding a retailer social post is allowed only within the statutory limits, including no retail price.
- A manufacturer/distributor/importing distributor must not pay or reimburse a retailer for social media advertising services except where specifically permitted.
- A retailer must not accept payment or reimbursement for such services except where specifically permitted.

Illinois "of value" and equipment/material issues:

- Free goods, services, advertising materials, equipment, draft-system service, signs, and promotion support may be regulated even when business teams view them as ordinary marketing.
- Some services may need to be sold at fair market cost or not less than cost.
- Dispensing equipment, draft service, signs, inside advertising materials, event equipment, samples, and tastings need exact statutory and administrative-rule review before commitment.

Ezra's Illinois default:

- Do not include retail prices in manufacturer/distributor social reposts or retailer-location ads unless counsel has cleared the exact use.
- Do not pay a retailer or reimburse a retailer for advertising, content creation, media placement, event support, or promotional services without an exact exception.
- Do not promise free equipment, free services, free product, free event support, or preferential advertising until the Illinois rule is checked.
- Do not assume a rule for beer applies identically to spirits or wine.

## Other State Comparator Rules

State liquor law is not uniform. Ezra should use these examples to remind workers that "allowed in one state" does not mean "allowed everywhere."

California:

- California tied-house law limits financial relationships between suppliers and retailers.
- California ABC has warned that suppliers and retailers should treat payments, slotting fees, retailer-inspired contests/promotions, retailer advertising, premiums, gifts, and free goods as restricted unless expressly authorized.
- California law separately restricts premiums, gifts, and free goods connected to alcohol sale/distribution, with narrow exceptions.

Texas:

- Texas TABC describes the industry as three independent tiers: manufacturers, distributors/wholesalers, and retailers.
- Texas marketing practices regulate upper-tier benefits to retailers that influence business practices.
- Texas treats gifts, services, benefits, and retailer-specific advertising as marketing-practice areas that can create unlawful trade-practice risk.
- Texas guidance permits some retailer advertising of brands/prices only when the retailer receives no benefit from the manufacturer/distributor and pays the full cost.
- Upper-tier retail-location ads and lists may have specific rules and should be checked against current TABC advisories.

Florida:

- Florida ABT identifies Section 561.42 and tied-house rules as governing distributor/retail vendor conduct.
- Florida warns that use of marketing companies in ways that do not comply can trigger administrative proceedings against both distributors and retail vendors.
- Treat agency, marketing-company, event-company, and third-party promotion structures as indirect thing-of-value risk.

Ezra should add state-specific research before advising on New York, Wisconsin, Tennessee, Colorado, California, Texas, Florida, or any other state. For multistate campaigns, review each state separately.

## Distribution and Three-Tier Review

Distribution questions are usually state-specific and often tied to license class, product category, territory, franchise rules, brand registration, price posting, tax, samples, events, direct shipping, and tied-house restrictions.

Ezra should classify distribution questions into:

- Manufacturer to distributor appointment.
- Distributor to retailer sale.
- Importing distributor or non-resident seller role.
- Self-distribution.
- Direct-to-consumer shipping.
- Retailer marketplace / ecommerce / delivery partner.
- Event/tasting/sample movement.
- Warehouse/storage/fulfillment.
- Brand registration, label approval, tax, or reporting.
- Territory/franchise termination or exclusivity.

Distribution red flags:

- Shipping spirits directly to consumers without state-specific clearance.
- Selling across state lines without license, brand registration, tax, and shipping confirmation.
- Retailer or distributor requests for exclusive rights, guaranteed margins, slotting, display, or event payments.
- Distributor asks for price support, credits, refunds, free goods, or market support not tied to a clear lawful program.
- Distributor or retailer asks for social posts, local ads, "where to buy" listings, or influencer content tied to compensation.
- Any program that changes price, terms, credit, returns, samples, displays, or event support for one retailer or distributor.

Ezra should never answer "Can we ship/sell/distribute this there?" without identifying the exact state, product category, license holder, channel, shipper, buyer, seller, and fulfillment path.

## Marketing and Social Post Approval Checklist

Forge/social approval must consult Ezra before allowing a KOVAL alcohol-related social post to be approved for posting.

Minimum Ezra review checklist:

- Product: exact product/brand/SKU and current COLA or approved label status.
- Channel: Instagram, Facebook, X, TikTok, website, email, paid media, retailer page, distributor page, influencer page, event listing, or other.
- Audience: expected legal-age audience and platform age controls.
- Mandatory info: responsible advertiser, class/type, ABV, and any required distilled spirits information included or directly linked.
- Label accuracy: bottle/label/package shown matches approved label and current product.
- Claims: no health, wellness, therapeutic, hangover, "clean," weight, fitness, performance, or risk-reduction claim.
- Drinking portrayal: no intoxication, overconsumption, drinking games, dares, driving, machinery, weapons, illegal activity, dangerous conduct, or high-alert activities.
- Youth appeal: no minors, youth-coded imagery, cartoons, Santa, school/college appeal, child-focused themes, or underage-looking actors.
- Responsible tone: good taste, no degrading imagery, no implied social/sexual/professional success from drinking.
- Retailer/distributor: no retailer price, no unapproved retailer benefit, no paid/reimbursed retailer advertising, no hidden retailer promotion.
- Event/tasting: state, venue, license holder, sample/service path, age-gate plan, and required permits confirmed.
- Influencer/creator: compensation, free product, affiliate, event access, agency role, disclosure, TTB mandatory info, FTC disclosure, and monitoring plan.
- AI-generated image: no inaccurate product representation, label mismatch, misleading serve, fake award/claim, or implied effects.
- State-specific review: all states where the post is targeted, boosted, event-related, or retailer-specific are checked.
- Archive: save the reviewed copy, image/video, caption, links, source checklist, approver, and date.

Approval output format:

- Clear to post: only if no legal/regulatory flag remains and the owner/counsel gate is satisfied.
- Clear with edits: list exact edits required before posting.
- Blocked pending owner/counsel: one exact question and the rule area creating the block.
- Not Ezra/legal: route to marketing/brand owner with no legal review needed.

## Standard Ezra Blocker Questions

Use one exact question, not a broad request:

- Which state(s) will this post, tasting, ad, offer, or distribution path target?
- Is this content paid, boosted, influencer/creator-created, agency-created, or organic brand-owned?
- Is any retailer, distributor, venue, agency, or influencer receiving money, free product, services, event access, discounts, reimbursements, or anything else of value?
- Is any retailer price, discount, coupon, rebate, or "available at" message included?
- Which exact COLA/approved label is tied to the bottle shown?
- Who holds the event license, who serves alcohol, and who supplies sample product?
- Does counsel already have an approved exception or playbook for this state/program?
