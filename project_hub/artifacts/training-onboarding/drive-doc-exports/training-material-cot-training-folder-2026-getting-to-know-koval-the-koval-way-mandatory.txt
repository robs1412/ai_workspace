﻿The KOVAL Way




What is Whiskey?


WHISK(e)Y: An alcoholic distillate made from a fermented grain mash, distilled at less than 190 proof, stored in oak containers, and bottled at no less than 80 proof.


All Bourbon is Whiskey, but not all Whiskey is Bourbon


The Bourbon ABC’s
* A - Made in America
* B - Aged in new American oak barrels w/ some level of char
* C - Must be at least 51% corn
* D - Distilled to a maximum of 160 proof, barreled at a maximum of 125 proof, and bottled at a minimum of 80 proof
* E - Except for water, bourbon cannot contain any additives, colorings, or flavorings


The Process


Here at KOVAL, we use alternative grains like Millet and Oat. We’re the first distillery in the world to ever distill Millet for whiskey (as white spirit is popular in Nepal), and it is the foundation of our Bourbon. We age all of our whiskeys in thirty-gallon barrels and age them for 2-4 years.


Distillation in a nutshell:


1. Milling: crushing or pressing the grain into a flour-like consistency
2. Mashing: softens starch cells. Imagine oatmeal-like consistency
3. Malting: converts non-fermentable starch in barley to fermentable sugar
   *  In some cases (any spirit made from 90% or more of a grain other than malted barley), enzymes are used to catalyze the breakdown of these starches.
4. Mashbill: A mash bill is simply a ratio of grains used in making whiskey.
   * By law, to be bourbon it has to be at least 51 percent corn; the remainder of that can be any grain you choose.
   * Traditionally, a lot of bourbons are going to run around 70 percent corn with the remainder made up between rye and malt. Our Bourbon is made up of 51% corn and 49% millet.
5. Fermentation: yeast converts sugar into alcohol, carbon dioxide, and heat.
   * Ours spend 4-6 days in the fermentation tanks.
6. Distillation: separates alcohol from water using heat.
   * 1. (10%) Heads: low-boiling alcohol (20-78°C // 68-174°F), mainly aldehydes and methanol.
      * We use this to clean our equipment.
   * 2. (60%) Hearts: pure ethanol, bright and palatable (80-94°C // 176-202°F).
      * The good stuff!
   * 3. (30%) Tails: higher-boiling alcohol (95-100°C // 203-212°F), fusel oils, “wet dog bouquet”.
      * Most of our liqueurs are made from redistilled rye tail cuts (we, of course, only use the heart cut from this redistillation).
7. Rectification: redistilling a distilled spirit (including neutral spirits)
8. Reduction (cutting): reducing alcoholic strength with water


How Does One Make Whiskey?


1. Freshly grind your grains with our Hammer Mill
2. Put that flour into our mash tank
3. Add water and Alpha Amylase
   *  Alpha Amylase is a natural enzyme that aids in the process of saccharification. Saccharification is the process of converting starches into sugar.
4. Put the sugary “mash” into our fermentation tank
5. Add yeast
6. Let it sit for 4 days.
   * Once the sugar has been digested by the yeast, the result is alcohol.
7. Once the mash has been fermented, it morphs into what we call, distiller’s beer. This is the foundation for all spirits.
8. We put our unfiltered distiller’s beer into the pot of the still. This means that we include parts of the grain in the mash instead of filtering them out. This results in a more flavorful spirit.
9. Collect the head, heart, and tail cuts


Head, Heart, & Tail: The Distillate 101


Head Cut: The first 10% that comes off the still
* It is mostly ethanol and acetone - essentially it’s a local, organic, and kosher nail polish remover! Poison! This is the reason why home distilling is illegal. Without the proper equipment, the head cut is not properly separated and it is difficult to differentiate between this and the heart cut.
What do we do with the head cut?
* We reuse this to clean all of our equipment in the distillery.


Heart Cut: This is the next 70% that comes off the still
* The good stuff!
* All of our products (whiskey, gin, brandy, etc.) are “heart cut”, meaning we only use the purest and best-tasting part of the distillate. In a practical sense, this cleaner whiskey allows us to get a great product out of our barrels in only two years. We put a better product into our barrels, so it does not need to, nor would it necessarily benefit from being aged for a longer period of time. Most large companies in the U.S. add tails to the hearts for their whiskey.


Tail Cut: The last 20% that comes off the still
* “Wet dog smell”
   * The smell comes from the Fusel oils left over from the distilling process.
* This is drinkable, however, KOVAL does not use this in our spirits.
* Some producers will mix this in with the heart cut and mask the smell and taste by aging it with artificial flavors.
   * If you get a hangover from a cocktail or two, chances are, you were drinking a product that had tails mixed into it.


What do we do with the tail cut?
* We re-distill them and take only the heart cut to make our liqueurs & vodka.
   * The more something is distilled, the more flavor gets stripped away- perfect for liqueurs and vodkas.


Our focus is to get the flavor out from the grain and not from the aging process. Most classic American whiskey is getting their flavor from the barrel, and KOVAL gets it from the grain itself. We are the only distillery with this focus worldwide.


The Barrel Breakdown


Koval barrels come from The Barrel Mill in Minnesota, and all of our barrels are New American White Oak Barrels.
* This is what needs to be used to label your spirits whiskey in the United States.
Our barrels are smaller than the industry standard, holding 30 gallons instead of 52.
* As a result, we don’t need to age our product as long.
* When you have a smaller surface area it takes less time to absorb all of the esters and caramel goodness in the barrels. We age our products between 2-4 years.
Currently, every whiskey at KOVAL is considered a Charred Whiskey because it was aged in a charred barrel.
* This means that a torch was placed inside the barrel to char it.
* Our barrels are considered a number 3 char, so the barrels are lit on fire for about 35 seconds.
* The levels of char range between 0-5. Zero being no exposure to heat and 5 being burned until white ash appears.


Why char our barrels?
1. It produces a literal lining of charcoal that can act as a great filtration system for tannins as the liquid floats through it over the years it spends in the barrel.
2. It caramelizes the wood sugars.


Let’s Talk Gin


To be considered a dry gin, a spirit has to be mostly juniper. Using our own White Rye as a base, KOVAL’s Dry Gin is 61% juniper and 12 other botanicals (grains of paradise, rose hips, angelica root, and coriander to name a few) that are proprietary. We can’t give away all of our secrets!


Additionally, our dry gin is the base for the Cranberry Gin and Barrel Aged Gin.


Fun fact: Gin is actually the world’s first flavored vodka! Gin and vodka are the same spirits; gin has just been distilled with a bouquet of botanicals.
* But not ours! Our base has some flavor and aroma as opposed to traditional vodkas.

How the Sausage Gin Gets Made


1. We start with a neutral grain base (typically Rye)
   * To get this base we do our normal whiskey making process and then take the hearts and/or tails and redistill them with all the plates engaged to get a much more neutral product.
      * This would be the only place in the process where we use the hammer mill and mash tank.
2. Grind the botanicals
   *  We measure out and grind out 13 botanicals fresh for each maceration. We use our botanical mill to mill the botanicals into a fine powder, almost the consistency of slightly moist sand.
3. Macerating
   * We pump water and our neutral base into one of our fermenters (just think of it as a tank, no fermentation happening here), to which we will add the ground up botanicals.
4. We let the maceration sit overnight, usually about 16-18 hours.
   * This is where the botanicals meet the alcohol, so no, the botanicals do not go through the mashing process. (Hammermill, mash tanks, fermentation)
5. Distillation
   * After the maceration has sat overnight, we pump it over to our still and distill the maceration without taking out or adding anything. The botanicals stay in the still and heat up, which also help to extract a lot of flavors. We then collect only the best part of the distillate, the heart cut, as we do for all of our products.
6. Cutting and Proofing
   * Once we have the hearts collected, we will add however much water we need for barreling or for bottling.
7. Gin gets bottled or barreled
