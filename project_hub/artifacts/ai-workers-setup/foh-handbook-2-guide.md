# Organic & Kosher

## KOVAL Cheat Sheet: Organic & Kosher Certifications

At KOVAL, our commitment to quality is backed by rigorous third-party certifications. Being Certified Organic and Certified Kosher isn't just about labels; it’s about the purity of our process and our "grain-to-bottle" philosophy.

### 1. Certified Organic (USDA Organic)

KOVAL spirits are made using 100% organic grains sourced from local farmers.

- What it means: Our grains are grown without synthetic pesticides, herbicides, or chemical fertilizers.
- The KOVAL Difference: Most distilleries purchase "GNS" (Grain Neutral Spirit) from large industrial factories. We mash, and ferment organic grain from scratch right here in Chicago.
- The Flavor Benefit: Organic grains provide a brighter, cleaner, and more "true-to-grain" flavor profile. Because we don't use chemicals in the farming process, the natural character of the millet, oat, corn, and rye shines through.
- The Certification: Our facility and products are certified by Midwest Organic Services Association (MOSA).
### 2. Certified Kosher (OU)

Nearly all KOVAL spirits are Certified Kosher by the Orthodox Union (OU), one of the most respected kosher certification agencies in the world.

- What it means: Every step of our production—from the grains and yeast we use to the cleaning agents for our stills—has been inspected to ensure it meets strict Jewish dietary laws. Yet this is a great certification for everyone because it is an independent certifier checking to make sure we are doing what we say we are doing.
- Why it matters: For many guests, a Kosher seal is a "Super-Certificate" of cleanliness and transparency. It guarantees that there are no hidden additives or cross-contamination in the distillery. No bug colorings, etc.
- Kosher for Passover: None of our spirits are currently Kosher for Passover (that requires a different process that would exclude grains).

### 3. Important Exceptions

While almost everything we make is Organic and Kosher, there is one major exception for staff to remember:

- KOVAL RTDs (Canned Cocktails): Our canned cocktails are Certified Organic, but they are NOT Certified Kosher. This is because they are canned at an off-site facility that does not hold a Kosher certification.
- Susan for President: Our brandy may not always be organic due to where we’re able to source fruit and ingredients.

### Staff Talking Points (How to tell the story)

If a guest asks "Why organic?":

"We source all of our grain from local organic farmers. It’s better for the environment, but more importantly for you, it results in a much cleaner, brighter-tasting spirit because there are no synthetic chemicals or pesticides involved in the process."

If a guest asks "Is this Kosher?":

"Yes! All of our bottled spirits are OU Kosher certified. It’s a testament to how clean our facility is and the transparency of our ingredients. The only items that aren't Kosher-certified are our canned cocktails because they are packaged off-site."

The "Heart Cut" Connection:

"Because we use organic grains and only take the 'heart cut' of the distillate—which is the cleanest part of the run—you're getting the highest quality spirit possible. It’s grain-to-bottle in its purest form."

# Meal & Rest Breaks Policy

## Meal & Rest Breaks Policy

At KOVAL, we value the hard work you put into every shift. To keep our team sharp, safe, and hospitable, it is essential that you take your allotted downtime. We follow strict guidelines to ensure everyone gets their rest and that we stay in compliance with labor laws.
### 1. Mandatory Unpaid Meal Breaks

All non-exempt (hourly) employees are required to take a 30-minute unpaid meal break for every 7.5 hours worked.

- Timing: Your break must be taken by at least the fifth hour of your shift.
- Recording: You are responsible for recording your meal breaks
### 2. Food Storage & Personal Items

To maintain health code standards and a professional appearance behind the bar, personal food storage is strictly regulated:

- The Fridge: All personal food, snacks, and leftovers must be stored in the fridge in the back break area.
- Behind the Bar: DO NOT store personal food, Tupperware, or drinks in the low-boys or ice wells behind the bar. Bar refrigeration is for service ingredients and garnishes only.
- Labeling: Please label your food with your name and the date.
### 3. General Break Expectations

- Personal Business: All personal business (including phone use, personal calls, or errands) must be conducted only during your scheduled break time.

In a high-volume distillery environment, timing is everything. Follow these protocols to ensure the floor stays covered:

- Communicate: Never simply walk off the floor. Coordinate with your fellow bartender or FOH team to find a "lull" in service before starting your break.
- The Hand-Off: Give a 60-second "State of the Union" to the person covering you. Mention any open tabs, pending orders, or guests who are "next" in line.
- Step Away: To truly recharge, take your break in the back office or a designated staff area. Avoid taking breaks in guest-facing areas (like the bar or patio) to prevent guests from attempting to order from you while you are off-the-clock.

# Age Verification & Identification

## KOVAL Policy: Age Verification & Identification

In accordance with Illinois law and the KOVAL Bar Emergency Action Plan, we maintain a strict "Card Everyone" policy to ensure responsible service. As a craft distillery, we are a high-visibility establishment; protecting our license and our community is everyone's responsibility.

### The "Under 40" Rule

Policy: You must require any patron who appears to be under 40 years of age to present a valid, government-issued photo identification.

- When to ask: Before the first drink is poured or at the point of entry for ticketed events.
- The Goal: If there is even a 1% doubt that a guest is 21, you must ask for an ID. It is always better to be safe than to face the legal consequences of serving a minor.

### How to Verify an ID

- Hold the ID: Physically take the ID from the guest. Do not just look at it through a wallet window.
- Check the Date: Look for the "Under 21 Until" date. If today’s date is before that date, they cannot be served.
- Verify the Person: Compare the photo to the person standing in front of you. Check height, eye color, and facial structure.
- Check for Authenticity: Feel for any peeling edges, unusual thickness, or bumps that suggest a photo has been swapped. Look for the holographic overlays required by the state.

### Approved Forms of Identification

We only accept current (unexpired), government-issued photo IDs:

- US State Driver’s License or State ID
- US Military ID
- Valid Passport (US or Foreign)
- Permanent Resident Card (Green Card)

Note: We cannot accept "Vertical" IDs for alcohol service in many cases unless you have triple-checked the date and verified it with the Manager on Duty (MOD).

### The KOVAL "Carding" Scripts

Standard Request:

"I’d love to get that drink started for you! May I just see your ID real quick? We card everyone who looks under 40."

If the ID is Expired:

"I’m so sorry, but I can’t accept this ID because it expired on [Date]. Per Illinois law, I need a current, valid photo ID to serve you alcohol today."

If they didn't bring an ID:

"I completely understand it’s frustrating, but because we are a distillery, I legally cannot serve you alcohol without a physical ID on hand. You are more than welcome to stay and enjoy a soda or some snacks, though!"

### Handling Suspicious or Fake IDs

If an ID appears forged, tampered with, or does not match the guest:

- Stay Calm: Do not accuse the guest of a crime.
- Refusal: If the team confirms it is invalid, we will refuse service. Do not attempt to confiscate the ID unless instructed by law enforcement.

So let's talk about how we turn these locally sourced grains into a delicious distillate.  It’s important to be using really high-quality ingredients from the start, as this will greatly affect the finished product!  On the Classic Tour, you will learn a little bit about our Gins, Whiskeys and our Vodka.  The easiest way to understand how we make these products, is to understand how we make the base.  KOVAL uses a rye base for our vodka, most of our gins and liqueurs, and we also make an incredibly delicious Rye Whiskey.  For the purpose of this tour, we will first go over how we make a rye distillate, and then we will talk about the additional steps we take for each specific category.

At KOVAL we are a Grain-to-Bottle distillery. (Fun fact, we actually have that phrase trademarked!) Many spirit brands out there actually purchase pre-made base spirits, rather than making it themselves, before adding their botanicals or aging in a barrel. (They don’t even have to tell you this! Pretty crazy).

Freshly grinding grains gives us more ownership, it's more economical for us to grind them ourselves - we know what happens to the grains.  Freshly milled grains also maintain a lot more flavor - kind of like fresh ground spices or coffee.

You do this by putting your grains in a Hammer Mill. Once you have ground your grains, you put that flour into our mash tank. You then add water and Alpha Amylase. It helps with liquefaction. We get all of our water from Lake Michigan.

Gluco Amylase is a natural enzyme that aids in the process of saccharification. Saccharification is the process of converting starches into sugar.

Having a sugary mash is important because you then put that mash into our fermentation tank, add yeast, and allow it to sit for 5-7 days.

This is why the process of saccharification is so important – you want to have a sugary mash because yeast is just like us – it loves to eat sugary things. Once that sugar is digested, the result is alcohol.

Below please find an in depth description of why we add alpha amylase, as opposed to malting. This is important to understand as a representative of KOVAL, but not something I recommend talking about on the tour. You may get the occasional question about this, which is why it is good to know.

Once we have our grains pulverized, we put it in the mash tank with the enzyme alpha amylase. Alpha amylase is naturally produced during the malting process. You might see the term “malting” on labels with other descriptions, such as smoked or toasted, so let’s go over what that means.

Malting is a process where you expose the grains to heat and humidity, exciting them to germinate. However, you need to stop the grains from germinating so that you don’t get little plants in your beer. To halt the germination process, grains are exposed to heat either through roasting, toasting, or smoking. This creates alpha amylase, crucial to the fermenting process.

For most of our products, we don’t use malted grains because the malting process takes away much of the grain flavor and replaces it with the process: smoked malted grains are going to taste less like grains and more like a smoked product. We want to highlight the unique flavors of the grains, so instead of malting, we simply incorporate the enzyme that is naturally created during that process.

Once the mash has been fermented, it morphs into this magical substance, which is called distillers beer. ( Hold up mason jar )

This is the foundation of our spirits.

For many years we used our 300L Kothe Still, but have since upgraded to a 5000L still, which you will see straight ahead. To go over how we distill this jar, please feel free to shift to this railing.

Move Group to the other side of railing that overlooks our distillation system

We put our unfiltered distiller’s beer into the pot of the still. This means that we use a “grain in mash,” because we include parts of the grain instead of filtering them out. This results in a more flavorful spirit.

We then cook the distiller’s beer to its boiling point. The distiller’s beer evaporates into a gas/vapor, which will become our spirit. The vapor then travels up into the whiskey helmet, and then down through a pipe that connects to our distillation columns.

Once the vapor makes its way into the first distillation plate, it is met with a room temperature. That temperature change causes it to turn back into a liquid.

However, it can’t stay that way for long, because the hot air from the pot and additional heating elements are still being pumped through the connected pipe as well as up into the first plate.

That hot gas causes the distillers beer to once again boil, and for the alcohol to once again evaporate and travel as a vapor into the next distillation plate. This process repeats until the gas reaches the top of the distillation column, and then through the pipe connecting it to our second distillation column. The vapor then falls to the bottom of the second column, and the process repeats. Once the vapor travels up through the second distillation column, it travels through the pipe connecting it to our condenser.

Inside the condenser, is a coiled copper pipe. Midway down, this copper pipe is surrounded by a cold water bath. This temperature change causes it to once again, to turn into a liquid and freely flow off the spout.

The distiller can then oversee what cut is coming off of the still through measuring the alcohol content with a hydrometer and also using their sense of smell.

The first cut to come off of the still is the heads. If you have ever heard the term “This will make you go blind,” this stuff will indeed make you go blind. It’s the first 10% of what comes off of the still, and is mostly methanol and acetone.  At the distillery, we use it to clean the floors and other hard to clean surfaces. This is poisonous and the reason that home distilling is illegal, and also the origin of moonshiney terms like “That stuff will make you go blind.”

Without the proper equipment, it is difficult to differentiate between this and our next cut- The Heart Cut!

The next cut to come off of the still is the good stuff -- the hearts. This is 60 - 70% of what comes off of the still, and it is the only cut we use in our spirits.

The last cut off of the still is the tails. It’s called tails not only because it is the last cut, but because it also tastes and smells like a wet dog. Its distinctive smell comes from the fusel oils left over from the distilling process.Some producers will mix in the tail cut in their product because the smell and taste can be calmed with aging and artificial flavors. However, we do not.

Before KOVAL came on the scene, this “heart cut” style of distilling wasn’t particularly popular for making whiskey or gin in America, because many producers felt like covering up the tails with aging or botanicals was a good enough option. But Robert grew up using this “heart cut” style in Austrian Eau d’Vie brandy making. You want the flavor of the ingredients (in that case, pure fermented fruit) to truly shine, so you only use the “heart cut.” So when KOVAL started making whiskey and gin, we figured - why not show that same respect for our beautiful grains? They all have such different flavors and characteristics - we wanted the grains to be the real star of the show!

Who here has had a REALLY BAD hangover, but you didn’t have a ton to drink?

Chances are, you were drinking a product that had tails mixed into it.

Gin for example, is a product that a lot of people might not like because they’ve had a bad experience with it, or as we like to say - have had a Gincident. This is because Gin is technically the world’s first flavored vodka, as it is a distilled spirit predominantly flavored with Juniper, and therefore a top offender for a lower shelf spirit with lots of tail cut.

The chemicals in the tails that make you dehydrated are called Congeners – they make you sick because your body processes them like they’re poison.

When we make gin, we work with 13 botanicals for a floral and fresh drinking experience. The same way we use a hammer mill to grind up our grains, we use a botanical mill to grind up our botanicals. With only 60% juniper, notable additional botanicals are green cardamom pods, coriander and rose hips.

We soak these ground up botanicals in our fresh distillate for an 18 hour maceration process. We then distill and bottle.

Barrel Talk

While we are currently in the process of experimenting with new cooperages, for many years we worked exclusively with The Barrel Mill, located in Minnesota. The whiskey that you sample today will be from this cooperage. All of our barrels are New American Charred Oak barrels.

We are also proudly a single barrel company, meaning each bottle can be traced back to the single, approved barrel it was aged in.

Our barrels have historically been smaller than industry standard, holding 30 gallons instead of 50 – 53. As a result, we don’t need to age our product as long, because when you have a small surface area, math is your friend, and it therefore takes less time to absorb all of the esters and caramel goodness in the barrels. If you have ever had the misfortune of drinking an over-oaked chardonnay, you know that is an undesirable outcome.

(Show number 3 char plank)

Every whiskey that you sample today has been aged in a Level 3 barrel. There are many positive reasons for charring a barrel. For starters, that level of heat will caramelize the wood sugars. It also creates a lining of charcoal, which is a great filtration system as the liquid floats over the years.

# Monarch 1130 Pricing Gun

Monarch 1130 Pricing Gun based on standard industry operations.

### 1. Setting the Price

- Pull the side knob out until the pointers/indicators are over the column (digit) you want to change.
- Turn the knob until the desired character or number appears in the viewing window.
  - Note: To print a blank space, look for the blank setting on that band.
- Push the knob all the way back in once the price is set. Never leave it pulled out while printing.
### 2. Loading Labels

- Open the Cover: Slide the two black latches on the sides toward the back of the gun. The cover will swing open.
- Prep the Roll: Tear off the first 10-12 inches of labels (about 25 labels) and discard them. This removes the adhesive from the starter tape that often causes jams.
- Position the Roll: Drop the label roll into the gray holders so the labels feed off the top of the roll.
- Thread the Gun: Drape the label strip across the top and over the metal "peel bar" at the front. Let about 6 inches hang out.
- Close the Cover: Snap it shut, ensuring the labels are centered.
- Feed the Backing Paper: Flip the gun upside down. Take the loose end of the labels and peel off the sticky labels so you just have the paper backing. Insert that paper into the slot above the handle (look for the two black arrows).
- Click to Engage: While holding the paper in the slot, squeeze the trigger repeatedly (about 8 times) until the internal wheels grab the paper and it starts feeding through the back of the gun.
### 3. Changing the Ink Roller

If the print is faint, do not press harder; change the ink.

- Open the top cover (same as loading labels).
- Locate the small ink roller at the front.
- Release: Most 1130 models have a small release button or latch. Press it, and the old roller should drop out (do this over a trash can to avoid getting ink on your hands).
- Insert: Take the new roller, slide it onto the spindle, and push until it clicks.

# Deliming Dishwasher

### KOVAL FOH Maintenance: Dishwasher Deliming Guide

Frequency: Once per month (or more if you see white scale/chalky buildup). Supplies Needed: Commercial Delimer/Descaler solution, rubber gloves, and safety glasses.

### Phase 1: Preparation

- Drain the Machine: Turn off the power or use the "Drain" switch to empty the wash tank completely.
- Clean the Filters: Remove the scrap trays and filter screens. Rinse them in the sink to remove any food debris or "gunk" before starting the chemical process. Replace them.
- Cut the Soap: To avoid mixing chemicals, turn off the detergent and sanitizer feed (usually a toggle switch on the chemical pump or the back of the machine). If there is no switch, simply pull the "Detergent" (Red) and "Sanitizer" (Clear) pickup tubes out of their buckets.
### Phase 2: The Deliming Cycle

- Refill with Fresh Water: Switch the machine back to "Fill" mode and let it fill with clean, hot water.
- Add Delimer: Open the door/hood. Pour the recommended amount of Delimer (usually 4–8 oz depending on the brand) directly into the wash tank water.
  - Note: Do not add regular soap or bleach during this step.
- Engage Delime Mode: Most commercial units have a dedicated “Delime/Run” switch (often located on the side or behind the front panel).
  - Flip the switch to DELIME.
  - Close the door. The machine will now run the pump continuously without timing out.
- Run for 20 Minutes: Let the machine circulate the acid solution for 15–20 minutes. This dissolves the lime and starch buildup inside the wash arms and on the heating elements.
- Soak (Optional): For heavy buildup, stop the machine halfway through and let it sit for 10 minutes to soak, then resume.
### Phase 3: Rinsing & Reset

- Drain & Flush: Once the time is up, flip the switch back to RUN and drain the tank completely.
- Rinse Cycle: Fill the machine with fresh water and run one standard "empty" cycle to flush out any remaining acid. Drain it once more.
- Restore Chemicals: Re-engage the detergent/sanitizer switches or put the pickup tubes back into their respective buckets.
- Prime the Lines: Run 1–2 more empty cycles to ensure the soap and sanitizer have pulled back into the machine before washing the first load of dishes.

### 💡 Pro-Tips for KOVAL Staff

- "The Sluggish Arm" Check: If you notice the wash arms aren't spinning freely, it’s usually because a seed or piece of glass is stuck in the nozzle. Unscrew the arms (usually a hand-tighten nut) and rinse them under the sink before deliming.
- Safety First: Delimer is an acid. Always wear gloves. If it splashes on the stainless steel exterior, wipe it off immediately with water to prevent spotting.
- When it’s Busy: Do not attempt to delime during service. This should be a closing task or a slow mid-shift task, as it takes the machine out of commission for about 30 minutes.
# Marble Care

# KOVAL Distillery: Marble Bar Care & Preservation Standards

Location: 4241 N Ravenswood Ave

Subject: Maintenance of White Marble Bar & Backbar

### OVERVIEW

The striking white marble bar and backbar are the centerpieces of our tasting room. Because marble is a porous, natural stone, it requires specific care to maintain its luster.

Most liquids served behind a bar (citrus juices, sodas, coffee, and spirits) are highly acidic. Acid reacts chemically with marble, causing immediate and often permanent damage. To ensure this bar remains a beautiful part of the guest experience for years to come, every team member must commit to working clean and being proactive in its maintenance.

### KEY TERMS TO KNOW

- Marble: A porous, metamorphic limestone. Unlike granite or synthetic quartz, it is highly reactive to chemicals.
- Etching: This occurs when acid (lemon juice, soda, wine) eats away at the stone’s finish. Etching looks like dull, cloudy white spots or "rings." These are not stains; they are physical damage to the stone that usually requires professional sanding to repair.
- Calcium Carbonate: The primary component of marble. It reacts with acid by decomposing—the same way an antacid tablet dissolves in your stomach.
- StoneGuard: A specialized micro-film applied to the top surface of our bar. It acts as a semi-permeable shield that prevents surface stains while allowing the stone to "breathe."

### THE STONEGUARD PROTECTIVE SEAL

Our bar is protected by a StoneGuard micro-film with a 10-year warranty.

- Coverage: Applied only to the top horizontal surfaces of the front and back bars.
- Maintenance Contact: Mario Lemoncello (Lemoncello Stone) | Cell: 630-669-5711.
- Repair: Damage to the film is repairable in sections. Seams are sealed with epoxy to prevent moisture infiltration.

### CLEANING & MAINTENANCE PROCEDURES

Daily Cleaning:

- Use a clean, soft towel to wipe away spills immediately.
- For sticky residue, use a standard table-turner wipe or a mild, non-acidic soap solution.
- Avoid abrasive scrubbers or harsh chemical cleaners (like bleach or vinegar), which will destroy the sealant and the stone.

Preventing Physical Damage:

- Do Not Drag Metal: Never drag shakers, tins, or metal trays across the surface. This can tear the StoneGuard film.
- Glassware Safety: Be cautious with broken glass. A shard can easily scratch or puncture the protective layer.
- Edge Awareness: The StoneGuard film does not wrap around the edges. Do not slide heavy objects over the seams, as this can cause the film to "lift" over time.

### CRITICAL CHALLENGES & "WATCH-OUTS"

- Exposed Sides: The StoneGuard film is only on the top. The vertical sides and bullnose edges of the bar are raw marble. These areas are highly vulnerable to etching from citrus sprays or spilled drinks. Wipe the sides of the bar frequently.
- The "Picking" Risk: While rare, guests may occasionally pick at the edge of the film. If you see a guest fidgeting with the bar surface, politely redirect them or offer a coaster/napkin to cover the area.
- Compromised Protection: If you notice a scratch, bubble, or tear in the film, notify management immediately. A breach in the film allows acid to trap itself against the stone, accelerating damage.

### PRO-TIPS FOR THE FLOOR

DO:

- Use coasters or napkins for every drink served.
- "Work Clean": Wipe up citrus juice or soda splashes the second they hit the surface.
- Keep an eye on the "splash zone" where bartenders are cutting fruit or shaking drinks.

DON'T:

- Don't set "garnishing stations" (lemons/limes) directly on the marble; always use a cutting board or tray.
- Don't leave wet towels sitting on the marble for extended periods.
- Don't use the bar top as a tool-sharpening or heavy-prep surface.

A beautiful bar is the start of a beautiful experience. Thank you for helping us protect our home.
# Floor Care

# KOVAL Distillery: White Oak Floor Care & Preservation

Location: 4241 N Ravenswood Ave

Subject: Maintenance of White Oak Flooring

### OVERVIEW

Our tasting room features custom white oak flooring finished with a natural, matte Bona sealant. This finish provides a rustic, organic aesthetic that aligns with our "grain-to-bottle" brand. However, the light, natural seal makes scuffs, dirt, and debris highly visible.

To preserve the integrity of the wood and the beauty of our space, the floors must be maintained with specific tools and techniques. Standard "slop" mopping is not permitted on the white oak; we use a specialized Bona system to prevent warping and water damage.

### CLEANING PROCEDURES

1. Preparation & Sweeping

- Clear the Floor: Remove large debris or wet spills immediately using a soft cloth or paper towel.
- Soft Sweep: Use only a soft-bristled broom to sweep the entire area.
- Why? Small, sharp objects or grit trapped under a mop act like sandpaper and will scratch the Bona finish.

2. The Bona Mopping System

- Microfiber Only: Always use a clean microfiber mop head.
- Moisten, Don’t Soak: Lightly moisten the mop head with water. The floor should never be "wet," only "damp."
- Spot Cleaning: For stubborn or sticky spots, apply Bona Wood Floor Cleaner.
- Monitor the Mop: Periodically check the microfiber pad. Once it turns dark grey, replace it with a fresh, clean pad. Using a dirty pad simply spreads grime into the wood grain.

### CRITICAL AREAS OF CONCERN

Freshly Mopped Areas

- Drying Time: Avoid all foot traffic on mopped areas until they are 100% dry to prevent footprints.
- Service Hours: Do not use Bona cleaner during service hours; the scent can interfere with the sensory experience of spirit tasting.
- Application: Never pour Bona cleaner directly onto the wood floor. Apply it to the mop head or lightly mist the spot.

Behind the Bar

- Floor Mats: Remove mats and clean them separately (outside or in the mop sink area).
- Water Management: Use a standard mop (wrung out until nearly dry) to soak up standing water.
- The "Sticky" Rule: Only use the Bona system behind the bar for deep-cleaning sticky syrup spills or at the end of the night.

Moving Furniture / Trash

- The "Lift, Don't Slide" Rule: Even with protective pads, sliding furniture can trap grit and gouge the wood. Always pick up chairs and bar stools to move them.
- Don’t drag trash cans or trash across the floor
- Felt Sliders: All furniture is equipped with felt pads. If you notice a missing pad or a "scratchy" chair leg, notify a manager immediately to prevent floor damage.
- Guest Interaction: If you see a guest struggling to move a heavy table or chair, politely assist them by lifting the furniture for them.

### PRO-TIPS FOR THE FLOOR

DO:

- Greet spills the moment they happen. The longer liquid sits, the more likely the oak is to swell.
- Use a "dry" mindset—the less water used on wood, the better.
- Ensure the "Wet Floor" sign is visible but professional.

DON'T:

- Never use bleach, ammonia, or general all-purpose cleaners on the white oak.
- Never leave a soaking wet mop resting on the wood floor.
- Never use a vacuum with a "beater bar" or hard plastic wheels, as these can dent the oak.

A clean floor reflects a polished brand. Thank you for keeping our space pristine.
# Frosty Factory Slushee

# QUICK GUIDE: Frosty Factory 217A Maintenance

Location: KOVAL Tasting Room Proper cleaning ensures a consistent freeze and prevents leaks or motor damage.

### I. THE CLEANING PROCESS (Tuesdays / Weekly)

1. Empty & Rinse

- Turn the power switch to OFF.
- Drain all remaining product into a bucket.
- Fill the hopper with cool water and turn the switch to WASH. Let it circulate for 2–3 minutes, then drain. Repeat until the water runs clear.

2. Disassembly

- Remove the Faceplate: Unscrew the four thumb screws.
- Pull the Beater: Carefully slide the large beater assembly out of the freezing cylinder.
- Remove the Rear Seal: Reach into the back of the cylinder and pull out the black rubber rear seal.
- Disassemble the Faucet: Pull the pin and remove the handle and plunger from the faceplate.

3. Sanitize

- Wash all parts in warm, soapy water.
- Soak all components in a sanitizer solution (Stera-Sheen or equivalent) for at least 5 minutes. Do not use bleach, as it can damage the seals.

### II. LUBRICATION & REASSEMBLY (Crucial Step)

The machine will leak or squeak if not lubricated properly.

- The Rear Seal: Apply a generous coat of food-grade lubricant (Petro-Gel) to the inside and outside of the black rubber rear seal. Slide it back onto the metal shaft at the rear of the cylinder.
- The Beater: Slide the beater back in. Rotate it until it "clicks" into the motor drive at the back.
- The Faceplate O-Ring: Apply a thin film of lubricant to the large O-ring on the faceplate to ensure a vacuum seal.
- The Plunger: Lubricate the two small O-rings on the faucet plunger before sliding it back into the faceplate.

### III. REFILLING & START-UP

- Assemble: Secure the faceplate with the four thumb screws (finger-tight only).
- Add Mix: Pour your pre-mixed batch into the top hopper.
- Wait for the Fill: Wait until the liquid stops bubbling into the cylinder before turning the machine on.
- Set to AUTO: Flip the switch to AUTO (not Wash). It will take roughly 15–20 minutes to reach serving consistency.

### IV. TROUBLESHOOTING & TIPS

- "It's not freezing": Check the back of the machine. Ensure there is at least 6–12 inches of clearance for airflow. If the filter is dusty, the machine will overheat and stop freezing.
- "It's leaking from the front": The faceplate O-ring is likely dry or misaligned. Re-lubricate.
- "It's leaking from the bottom/center": This usually means the Rear Seal was installed without enough lubricant or is worn out.

ADA Requirements for Service AnimalsQuestions RegardingLegal Guidelines to Follow and Exercise

What is a service animal?Under the ADA, a service animal is defined as a dog that has been individually trained to do work or perform tasks for an individual with a disability.  The task(s) performed by the dog must be directly related to the person's disability.

What is the difference between a service animal and an emotional support animal?A service animal is trained to take a specific action when needed to assist the person with a disability.Examples: a person with diabetes may have a dog that is trained to alert him when his blood sugar reaches high or low levels. A person with depression may have a dog that is trained to remind her to take her medication. Or, a person who has epilepsy may have a dog that is trained to detect the onset of a seizure and then help the person remain safe during the seizure.An emotional support/therapy/companion animal provides comfort just by being with the person.  Under the ADA, Because they have NOT been trained to perform a specific job or task, they do NOT qualify as service animals.

What is the Illinois White Cane Law?The “Illinois White Cane Law” requires public facilities to allow guide dogs, hearing dogs, seizure response dogs, seizure alert dogs, and "support" dogs. The law doesn't define these terms, but says that these dogs must be in training or being specially trained to assist someone who is blind or has a visual disability, has a hearing impairment, has epilepsy or another seizure disorder, or has another physical disability.

Does anxiety comforting dogs count as a service animal?This can be a little tricky.  The ADA makes a distinction between psychiatric service animals and emotional support animals. If the dog has been trained to sense that an anxiety attack is about to happen and take a specific action to help avoid the attack or lessen its impact, that would qualify as a service animal. However, if the dog's mere presence provides comfort, that would not be considered a service animal under the ADA.

Are animals-in-training considered service animals?Under the ADA, animals must be already trained before it can be taken into public places (might be different in other states).

How can we approach someone to determine if a dog is a service animal?If it is not obvious that a dog is a service animal (i.e. no vest), employees are able to legally only ask the following questions:

- “Is the dog a service animal required because of a disability?” (this seems a little weird wording, you could also ask “Is your dog working?” to make it not seem too interfering)

- “What work/tasks has your dog been trained to perform?”

*Staff is NOT allowed to request any documentation, ask for the dog to demonstrate their task, or inquire about the nature of the persons disability*Staff should also make sure to approach the conversation with the patron in an appropriate manner.  Our job is to make sure we are following the rules, but also being considerate of our customers and their feelings/disabilities.

# Biohazard Cleanup: “The 5-Step Response”

### Biohazard Cleanup: “The 5-Step Response”

This protocol must be followed for any vomit or diarrhea incident in the Tasting Room, Store, or Restrooms.
#### 1. Secure the Area (Immediate)

- Clear the Zone: Immediately ask guests to move away from the area.
- Block it Off: Use chairs, "Wet Floor" signs, or stanchions to create a 25-foot "no-go" perimeter around the incident.
- Notify: Alert the Manager on Duty (MOD) immediately.
#### 2. Gear Up (Personal Protection)

- Locate the Biohazard/Body Fluid Cleanup Kit (kept in the [Insert Location, e.g., Janitor's Closet/Under Kitchen Sink]).
- Put on PPE: You must wear the disposable gloves, face mask, and apron provided in the kit. If the incident is large, use the shoe covers.
#### 3. Absorption & Removal

- Cover: Sprinkle the absorbent powder (Vomit Star/Sand) over the entire area to solidify the liquid and reduce odor.
- Scoop: Once solidified, use the disposable scoop and scraper to move the waste into the Red Biohazard Bag.
- Seal: Tie the first bag tightly.
#### 4. The "Deep Clean" (Disinfection)

- Note: Standard glass cleaner or "green" cleaners do not kill norovirus. You must use the bleach-based solution or the specific disinfectant in the kit.
- Spray: Saturate the area and the perimeter with the disinfectant.
- Wait: Let the disinfectant sit for at least 10 minutes (or the time specified on the bottle) to ensure all pathogens are killed.
- Wipe: Use disposable paper towels to wipe up the solution and place them in a second biohazard bag.
#### 5. Disposal & Reset

- Double Bag: Place the first red bag and all used PPE (gloves, apron) into a second heavy-duty trash bag.
- Out of Building: Take the waste directly to the external dumpster. Do not put it in the bar or kitchen trash cans.
- Wash: Wash your hands with soap and hot water for at least 30 seconds immediately after finishing.

### ## DOs and DON’Ts of Biohazards

| DO | DON’T |
| --- | --- |
| DO throw away any open food, garnishes, or ice within 25 feet of the incident. | DON’T use a mop or a vacuum. This spreads germs into the air or the mop head. |
| DO remain professional. If guests ask, simply say: "We’re just performing a deep-clean for everyone's safety." | DON’T take off your mask or gloves until the area is fully disinfected. |
| DO replace any items from the Cleanup Kit that were used so it’s ready for the next shift. | DON’T allow guests back into the area until the floor is completely dry. |

# Tasting Room: Bar Program

In addition to the technical science of slushies and core recipes, new bartending employees at KOVAL must be well-versed in our specific operational standards, technical cocktail programs, and brand philosophies to maintain our "Approachable Expert" service model.
## 1. Technical Service Programs

Our bar program is divided into distinct categories that require different technical approaches:

- Signature Classics: New hires must master the "North Star" drinks, such as the KOVAL Old Fashioned (stirred for 20–30 seconds for 20% dilution) and the 100% Rye Manhattan.
- Clarification & Science: Employees should understand the chemistry behind our Clarified Milk Punches (like the Pain Killer or Spa Day). This involves using milk to curdle and trap tannins, creating a silky mouthfeel and crystal-clear liquid.
- Carbonation Management: For the Highball & Spritz series, bartenders are taught to pour soda water before the spirit (e.g., Cranberry Gin Liqueur) to ensure integration without aggressive stirring that kills carbonation.
- The Syrup Program: Precision is required for house-made modifiers. This includes 1:1 simple and turbinado syrups and specialized botanical infusions like Allspice Simple Syrup, which must never be boiled to avoid extracting bitter tannins.
## 2. Brand Knowledge & Storytelling

Every bartender acts as a Brand Ambassador and must be able to deliver the "Elevator Pitch":

- The History: KOVAL was Chicago’s first distillery to open since the mid-1800s. We are one of the largest independent, woman-owned craft distilleries in the US.
- The "Heart Cut": We use only the cleanest, most aromatic 60% of the distillate (the heart), discarding the "heads" and "tails" to ensure a bright, floral profile and a cleaner finish.
- Certifications: All spirits (KOVAL and Thresh & Winnow) are USDA Certified Organic and OU Kosher. The only exception is the RTD canned cocktails, which are organic but not kosher.
- Single Barrel: Every bottle is from a single barrel and can be traced back to the specific Midwestern field where the grain was grown.
## 3. Critical Operational Standards

- Marble Care: Our white marble bar is highly reactive to acid. Bartenders must never set garnishing stations (lemons/limes) directly on the marble and must wipe away citrus or soda splashes immediately to prevent permanent "etching."
- Wood Floor Preservation: We use a specialized system on our white oak floors. Standard "slop" mopping is strictly prohibited to prevent warping; use only approved cleaning methods.
- Safety & ID Policy: We follow an "Under 40" rule, requiring physical, government-issued IDs for anyone appearing under that age.
- Biohazard Response: In the event of a bodily fluid incident, a strict 25-foot "no-go" perimeter must be established immediately, and only specific bleach-based solutions from the cleanup kit should be used.
## 4. Hospitality & Sales

- The "Bottle Match": If a guest enjoys a cocktail, bartenders should suggest the corresponding retail bottle (e.g., Cranberry Gin) for them to take home.
- The Discount: Remind tour guests they receive 10% off retail items on the day of their visit.
- "Move to Yes": If a guest is unhappy with a drink, replace it immediately to maintain a premium guest experience.

  - Glassware/Garnish: Rocks glass over a large cube. Freshly grated nutmeg.

  - Ingredients: KOVAL Citrine (Thresh & Winnow), Lemon, Lavender Flower, Simple Syrup.

Focusing on carbonation and temperature management.

- Cranberry Gin Spritz

  - Glassware/Garnish: Collins glass. Lime wheel.

### Technical Note on Scaling (Batching)

For high-volume service at the Tasting Room, we use a Batch Factor. To scale any of the above (excluding the clarified drinks):

- Calculate total volume per drink (e.g., a Manhattan is 3.0 oz).
- Add 20% water to the batch to account for the dilution that would normally occur during stirring.
- Bottle and keep at 32°F - 34°F for a "pour-and-serve" program that maintains the integrity of a hand-stirred drink.

# Bar Tools

To work at the level of precision we demand at KOVAL, you must treat your bar tools as an extension of your craft. We don't just "pour drinks"; we manage dilution, aeration, and temperature.

Here is your guide to the essential kit and how we utilize it to showcase our "heart cut" spirits.

## 1. The Jigger (The Tool of Integrity)

At KOVAL, we never free-pour. We use Japanese-style jiggers for their height and accuracy.

- The Technique: Always pour to the very brim (the meniscus) to ensure a full measurement. Hold the jigger close to the mixing glass or tin to minimize spills.
- Why it matters: Our spirits are complex. An extra $0.25\text{ oz}$ of syrup can completely mask the delicate floral notes of our Millet Whiskey. Consistency is the hallmark of a professional.
## 2. Shaker Tins (The Aerators)

We use a weighted Boston Shaker (a large 28 oz tin and a small 18 oz tin).

- The Technique: Add your ingredients to the small tin first (cheapest to most expensive). Add ice to the large tin, then combine. Shake vigorously for about 12 seconds or until the tin ices over on the outside.
- Why it works: Shaking is for cocktails with citrus, dairy, or egg whites (like the Bee’s Knees). It introduces air bubbles, creating that essential frothy texture and rapid chilling.
## 3. Mixing Glass & Bar Spoon (The Dilution Duo)

For spirit-forward classics like the KOVAL Old Fashioned or Rye Manhattan.

- The Technique: Use a weighted bar spoon with a spiral handle. Place the back of the spoon against the interior wall of the glass and move the ice in a fluid, silent circle. Stir for 20–30 seconds.
- The Science: Stirring chills the drink while maintaining a silky, crystal-clear texture without the aeration or "bruising" caused by shaking.
## 4. The Strainers

We use three distinct types of strainers depending on the drink’s texture.

- Hawthorne Strainer: The "workhorse." Its spring fits inside the shaker tin to keep ice back. Use the "finger tab" to adjust the gate; a tighter gate prevents small ice chips from entering the glass.
- Julep Strainer: A perforated bowl used exclusively with the Mixing Glass. It fits at an angle to allow for a clean, elegant pour of stirred cocktails.
- Fine Mesh (Double) Strainer: Held beneath the Hawthorne or Julep strainer.
  - Mandatory for: Anything shaken with citrus, fruit purees, or matcha (to catch ice shards and pulp).
  - Reasoning: This ensures the final cocktail is perfectly smooth and looks premium in the glass.
## 5. The Refractometer (The Slushie Scientist)

This is perhaps our most unique tool behind the bar.

- The Use: Used to measure the Brix (sugar content) of our frozen batches.
- The Technique: Place a few drops of the room-temperature batch on the prism, close the cover, and look through the eyepiece.
- The Standard: If it isn't between 13% and 15%, it doesn't go in the machine.
## 6. Citrus Zesters & Peelers

- The Swath: We use Y-peelers to get wide, consistent swaths of orange or lemon.
- The Expression: Always "express" the oils by pinching the peel over the glass. This releases the aromatics that hit the guest before they even take a sip.
- Pro Tip: Never set your garnishing tools or citrus directly on our marble bar top—the acid will etch the stone. Always use a cutting board or a bar mat.
## 7. The Bar Blade & Wine Key

- Bar Blade: Primarily for opening our RTD Canned Cocktails and soda waters efficiently.
- Wine Key: Essential for opening our sweet vermouths and sparkling wine toppers for the Cran Gin Spritz.

### Maintenance & Care

- Tin Rinsing: Use the glass rinser between every build to prevent flavor cross-contamination.
- Polishing: Glassware must be polished with a microfiber cloth while still warm from the dishwasher to prevent water spots.
- Sanitization: All metal tools must be deep-cleaned at the end of every shift to prevent the buildup of sugar and acids.
# Using Jiggers

At KOVAL, we approach bartending with the same rigor our distillers use at the still. While free-pouring might look flashy in a high-volume dive bar, it has no place in a craft distillery environment. We always use a jigger for three primary reasons: Integrity of the Spirit, Consistency for the Guest, and Operational Precision.

Here is why we never guestimate a pour:
### 1. Honoring the "Heart Cut"

Our distillers go to great lengths to capture only the "heart cut"—the cleanest, most aromatic 60% of the distillate. KOVAL spirits (like our Millet Whiskey or Dry Gin) are known for their delicate, floral, and nuanced profiles.

- The Reason: If you free-pour and accidentally add an extra $0.25\text{ oz}$ of simple syrup or a "heavy" splash of citrus, you have effectively buried the spirit. We measure to ensure the grains we worked so hard to harvest and distill remain the star of the show.
### 2. Consistency: The Professional’s Hallmark

A guest should be able to order an Old Fashioned at 4:00 PM on a Tuesday and have it taste identical to the one they order at 11:00 PM on a Saturday.

- The Reason: Free-pouring is subject to human error, fatigue, and distraction. By using a jigger, you remove the "variable" of the bartender. We want our guests to fall in love with the recipe, not a specific bartender's "heavy hand."
### 3. Cocktail Math & Balance

Cocktail creation is chemistry. Our recipes are balanced down to the quarter-ounce to achieve a specific equilibrium between sweet, sour, bitter, and boozy.

- The Reason: A drink that is $0.25\text{ oz}$ off in any direction isn't just "a little different"—it is out of balance. It might be too cloying, too acidic, or too spirit-forward. Measuring ensures the ABV (Alcohol by Volume) and Brix (sugar content) stay exactly where they were designed to be.
### 4. Inventory and Accountability

As a distillery, we are accountable for every drop of spirit we produce.

- The Reason: Accurate pouring allows us to track our inventory with precision. Over-pouring by just $0.25\text{ oz}$ across 100 cocktails is nearly a full bottle of whiskey "lost" to the drain. Proper measurement ensures that our retail and bar operations remain sustainable and professional.
### 5. Hospitality Through Expertise

There is a common misconception that free-pouring is faster. In reality, a skilled bartender with a Japanese-style jigger is just as fast, but significantly more graceful.

- The Reason: Measuring shows the guest that you care about their specific drink. It demonstrates that you are a technician and an expert who respects the ingredients. It builds trust.

The KOVAL Rule: Build with the cheapest ingredients first (syrups, then citrus, then modifiers) and the spirit last. If you mis-measure a syrup into your jigger, you can toss it and start over without wasting our organic "heart cut" spirits.
# Double Straining

To maintain the premium quality of a KOVAL cocktail, double straining (also known as fine straining) is a mandatory technical standard for specific builds. It is the process of using two strainers simultaneously to ensure the most refined texture possible.
### What is it?

When pouring a cocktail from your shaker or mixing glass, you hold a standard Hawthorne or Julep strainer over the tin/glass as usual. However, you also hold a Fine Mesh Strainer directly over the serving glass so the liquid must pass through both before reaching the guest.
### Why do we do it? (The Science of Texture)

1. Eliminating "Ice Shards" When you shake a cocktail vigorously (as we do for a Bee’s Knees or an Espresso Martini), the ice collides with the metal walls and shatters into tiny splinters.

- The Problem: Without a fine strainer, these shards end up in the glass. They create an unpleasant "crunchy" texture and, more importantly, they provide extra surface area that causes the drink to over-dilute rapidly.
- The KOVAL Standard: A cocktail served "up" (in a coupe or martini glass) should be silky and uniform from the first sip to the last.

2. Removing Pulp and Solids Many of our recipes use fresh citrus, house-made fruit syrups, or botanical infusions (like our Matcha Gin Slush or Blackberry Syrup).

- The Problem: Citrus squeezers often drop small bits of pulp, and house-made syrups can contain tiny fruit fibers or botanical sediment.
- The KOVAL Standard: Double straining catches these particles, ensuring the drink is aesthetically clear and has a consistent mouthfeel.

3. Enhancing the Head (Aeration) For drinks involving egg whites or aquafaba (like our Clover Club or Blueberry Sour), the fine mesh strainer helps break up any large, uneven bubbles.

- The Result: It produces a tighter, more "velvety" foam head that supports garnishes better and looks more professional.

### When is it Mandatory?

As a rule of thumb at KOVAL, you must double strain if the drink is:

- Shaken and served "Up" (e.g., any Coupe drink).
- Contains fresh fruit or herbs (to catch seeds, pulp, or mint bits).
- Contains Matcha or solids (to prevent a "chalky" finish).
### Technique Tip:

Hold the fine mesh strainer about an inch above the glass. Pour the cocktail through the center of the mesh. If the mesh gets clogged by pulp, gently tap the side of the strainer with your shaker tin to speed up the flow—never use your fingers to push liquid through, as this compromises sanitation and texture.
# Other Bar Techniques

To round out the KOVAL training curriculum, we must address the specific "advanced" techniques that define our craft program. These methods bridge the gap between simple drink-making and true spirits science.

Here are the essential bar methods we should outline for the team:
### 1. Clarification (Milk Punch Method)

This is our signature method for high-volume, elegant cocktails like the Pain Killer or Spa Day.

- The Method: We combine spirits, juices, and sweeteners, then pour that mixture into a container of whole milk. The acid in the juice curdles the milk; as the curds settle, they act as a natural filter, stripping away tannins and color.
- The Goal: To create a crystal-clear, shelf-stable liquid with a rich, velvety mouthfeel.
- Training Note: New hires must learn that we always add acid to milk, never milk to acid, to ensure a controlled curdling process.
### 2. The "Pre-Dilution" Batching Method

For our high-volume "Draft" or "Ready-to-Pour" cocktails, we simulate the stirring process on a large scale.

- The Method: When batching a drink like our Old Fashioned, we calculate a 20-25% dilution rate and add filtered water directly to the batch.
- The Goal: This allows us to serve a spirit-forward cocktail at the perfect dilution and temperature instantly, without needing to stir for 30 seconds every time.
- Training Note: This requires "Cocktail Math." For example, 25.4 oz of water is added to a 101 oz bourbon batch to hit that perfect sweet spot.
### 3. Proper Carbonation Management (The "Anti-Stir")

For our Cranberry Gin Spritz and Highball programs, the method of assembly is the most important factor.

- The Method: We teach the "Soda First" or "Layered" build. Pour the soda water into the glass first or over the ice, then slowly add the spirit and citrus.
- The Goal: This creates a natural "density integration" where the heavier spirit sinks through the bubbles, mixing the drink automatically.
- Training Note: Aggressive stirring with a bar spoon kills the CO2. We want a "quick lift" with the spoon to ensure the bottom of the glass is incorporated without losing the effervescence.
### 4. Expression & Aromatic Application

The guest tastes with their nose before their tongue.

- The Method: We use a "Swath" of citrus peel. The bartender must express the oils (the "spritz") specifically over the top of the glass, then rub the rim of the glass with the peel before inserting it.
- The Goal: To capture the volatile oils of the fruit, providing an immediate sensory bridge to the botanicals in our organic gin or the grain profile of our whiskeys.
- Training Note: For the Frozen Coffee, we apply this same logic by expressing lemon oil over the frozen surface to brighten the heavy oat and cold brew notes.
### 5. Glassware Tempering

- The Method: Every "Up" drink (served in a coupe or martini glass) must be served in a chilled vessel. If the glass isn't coming out of a freezer, it must be "flash-chilled" using ice and soda water while the drink is being prepared.
- The Goal: To maintain the temperature of the cocktail for as long as possible, as these drinks do not have ice to keep them cold.
- Training Note: Always dump the tempering ice and water just before straining to ensure the glass is at its absolute coldest.
### 6. The "Dry Shake" vs. "Reverse Dry Shake"

Essential for cocktails with egg whites or aquafaba, like our Clover Club or Blueberry Sour.

- The Method: * Dry Shake: Shake all ingredients without ice first to emulsify the protein, then add ice and shake again to chill.
  - Reverse Dry Shake: Shake with ice first, strain out the ice, and shake again without ice.
- The Goal: To create a thick, meringue-like foam head that sits perfectly on top of the liquid.
- Training Note: This requires extra "elbow grease." A weak shake results in a flat, unappealing drink.
# Syrup Program

### The KOVAL Syrup Program: A Technical Guide

At KOVAL, our syrups are designed to complement the unique grain profiles of our spirits. We prioritize consistency through precise measurements and controlled temperature management.

### 1. Core Sweeteners (1:1 Ratios)

These are the foundational building blocks for our classic builds.

- Simple Syrup
  - Ratio: 1:1 by weight.
  - Ingredients: 64 oz White Sugar, 64 oz Water.
  - Method: Combine and heat until the sugar is fully dissolved. Chill quickly to maintain Brix consistency and store in the refrigerator.
- Turbinado (Turbo) Syrup
  - Ratio: 1:1 by weight.
  - Ingredients: 64 oz Sugar in the Raw, 64 oz Water.
  - Method: Heat until fully dissolved. Note that Turbinado sugar has a higher mineral content and takes longer to dissolve than white sugar. Use this for spirit-forward cocktails like the KOVAL Bourbon Old Fashioned.
- Honey Syrup
  - Ratio: 2:1 by weight.
  - Ingredients: 80 oz (5 lbs) Honey, 40 oz Hot Water.
  - Method: Stir thoroughly until the honey is fully integrated with the hot water.

### 2. Botanical & Infused Syrups

Precision in temperature is critical here to avoid extracting bitter tannins from dry spices.

- Allspice Simple Syrup
  - Ingredients: 32 oz Simple Syrup, 32 oz Turbo Syrup, 2 cups Allspice berries, 6 cinnamon sticks, ¼ nut of grated nutmeg.
  - Method: Combine over low heat. Do not boil. Keep the mixture hot for 1–2 hours to extract oils. Store in the fridge with spices overnight, then strain through cheesecloth.
- Almond Orgeat
  - Ingredients: 1.5 Cups raw almonds, 1.5 Cups warm distilled water, 1 Cup white sugar, 2 Tbsp Vodka (for stabilization), 1 tsp Almond extract, 2 tsp Orange blossom water, ⅓ tsp Vanilla extract, 1 microplaned orange.
  - Method: Warm water and dissolve sugar. Add remaining ingredients and simmer on low for 10 minutes. Cool for 30 minutes, then chill for 24 hours. Fine strain and bottle.
- Barley Tea Syrup
  - Ingredients: 64 oz Hot water, 64 oz Sugar, 2 barley tea bags.
  - Method: Steep tea in hot water for 5–8 minutes. Remove bags, add sugar, and stir until dissolved.

### 3. Fruit & Savory Syrups

These syrups utilize whole ingredients and require mechanical emulsification and fine filtration.

- Blackberry / Strawberry Syrup
  - Ingredients: 7.5 Cups White sugar, 7.5 Cups Water, 7.5 Cups Fruit (blended with a small amount of water).
  - Method: Bring to a boil, then reduce heat and simmer for 15 minutes. Pass through a cheesecloth and strainer, pressing the solids to extract all pigment and juice.
- Marshmallow Syrup
  - Ingredients: 64 oz White sugar, 32 oz Marshmallows, 3 Tbsp Vanilla extract, 4 tsp Lemon juice.
  - Method: Combine over heat and simmer for 15–20 minutes. Mix thoroughly and allow to cool completely before bottling.
- Serrano Syrup (Spiced)
  - Ingredients: 128 oz White sugar, 128 oz Water, 16 Serrano peppers (ends removed).
  - Method: Heat until sugar is dissolved and peppers have infused. Strain carefully.

### Technical Standards

- Dilution Control: Always allow syrups to reach room temperature or lower before using them in a cocktail to ensure your dilution math remains accurate during the shake or stir.
- Shelf Life: Infused syrups (Orgeat, Fruit) should be kept refrigerated and used within 2 weeks for peak freshness. Foundation syrups (Simple, Turbo) are stable for up to 4 weeks if stored properly.
# Slushee Program

Creating a successful frozen batch requires a delicate balance of alcohol, water, and sugar. If your sugar levels or ABV (Alcohol by Volume) are off, you'll end up with either a rock-solid block of ice or a watery, unfrozen mess.
## The Science of the Slush: Understanding Brix

The most critical factor in a frozen drink is the Brix level—a measurement of the sugar content in a solution.
### Why Brix Matters

Sugar acts as an anti-freeze. Without enough sugar, the water in your batch will freeze into a solid mass that can break the machine's auger. Too much sugar, and the mixture will never reach a frozen state.
### How to Measure and Adjust

- The Tool: Use a Refractometer to measure the Brix of your final batch before pouring it into the machine.
- The Target: For most commercial slushie machines, you are aiming for a Brix level between 13% and 15%.
- Adjusting: * If Brix is too low: Add more sugar (simple syrup or liqueur).
  - If Brix is too high: Add more water or juice.

## Batching Fundamentals

To ensure your machine runs smoothly, follow our standard 11-unit batch template:

| Component | Parts | Role |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Liquor | 2 Parts | Provides the base spirit (Aim for ~10% total ABV). |
| Sweetener | 1.5 Parts | Controls the Brix and freeze point. |
| Citrus/Juice | 1 Part | Provides acid and brightness. |
| Dilution (Water) | 4 Parts | Essential for crystal formation and texture. |

- Pre-Chill Everything: Always chill your batch in the walk-in before adding it to the machine. This reduces the "recovery time" and puts less strain on the cooling compressor.
